\section{$\phi^4$-Ising connection}

The {\bf Ising model} partition function is

\beq
Z = \prod_i\left(\sum_{{S_i=\pm 1}}\right) \exp\left(\beta J \sum_{(i, j)\in NN} S_i S_j
+\beta H\sum_i S_i
\right)
\label{eq-ising-part-fun-z}
\eeq

\begin{claim}($\phi^4$-Ising connection)
\label{cl-p4-ising-connection}

The Ising model partition function $Z$
given by Eq.(\ref{eq-ising-part-fun-z})
can be approximated by
\beq
Z= \int \cald\phi\; e^{-\cala}
\eeq
where
the action $\cala$  is given by

\beq
\cala[\phi] \approx \int d^2 x \left[\frac12 \sum_{\mu=1}^2
(\partial_\mu \phi)^2 + \frac{m^2}{2}\phi^2 + \frac{ g}{4!}\phi^4
+ h\phi\right]
\label{eq-ginz-landau-act}
\eeq
Eq.(\ref{eq-ginz-landau-act}) defines the {\bf Landau-Ginzburg model}.
\end{claim}
\proof


Assume $H=0$ to begin with.


Rewrite the spin-spin interaction in matrix form:

\beq
\beta J\sum_{(i,j)\in NN} S_i S_j = \frac12 \sum_{i, j} S_i K_{ij} S_j =\frac12 S^T K S
\eeq
Assume that the spins $S_i$
are continuous functions of 
the Cartesian coordinates $(x,y)$
and that the lattice spacing is $a$
both in the $x$ and $y$ directions.
Then

\beq
\begin{array}{ccc}
\bcen
\xymatrix@R=1pc@C=1pc{
S_1
&S_2
&S_3
\\
S_4
&S_5
\ar@{-}[r]
\ar@{-}[l]
\ar@{-}[u]
\ar@{-}[d]
&S_6
\\
S_7
&S_8
&S_9
}
\ecen
&\rarrow
&
\bcen
\xymatrix@R=1pc@C=.1pc{
&\scriptstyle(1+a\partial_y+ \frac{a^2}{2}\partial^2_y)S_5
&
\\
\scriptstyle(1-a\partial_x+ \frac{a^2}{2}\partial^2_x)S_5
&S_5
\ar@{-}[r]
\ar@{-}[l]
\ar@{-}[u]
\ar@{-}[d]
&\scriptstyle (1+a\partial_x+ \frac{a^2}{2}\partial^2_x)S_5
\\
&\scriptstyle(1-a\partial_y+ \frac{a^2}{2}\partial^2_y)S_5
&
}
\ecen
\\
\\
\beta J S_5(S_4 +  S_6 
+ S_8 + S_2)
&\approx
&
\beta J S_5 \left[
4 +a^2(\partial_x^2 + \partial_y^2)
\right]S_5
\end{array}
\eeq


\beq
\frac{K}{2}= 4\beta J (1 +\frac{a^2}{4}\sum_\mu \partial_\mu^2 )
\implies K^{-1}=\frac{1}{8\beta J}
\frac{1}{1 +\frac{a^2}{4}\sum_\mu \partial_\mu^2 }\approx
\frac{1}{8\beta J}\left(
1 -\frac{a^2}{4}\sum_\mu \partial_\mu^2\right)
\eeq
The Gaussian integral formula
over one degree of freedom (dof) $x\in \RR$

\beq
\int_{-\infty}^\infty
dx\;e^{-a x^2 + bx}=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}
e^{\frac{b^2}{4a}}
\eeq
generalizes to 
the following
formula when integrating
over $N$ dofs $\phi(x_i)=\phi_i\in \RR$
for $i=1,2, \ldots, N$.

\beq
\int \underbrace{\cald\phi}_{\prod_i d\phi_i} \;
\exp\left(-\;\frac{1}{2}\phi^T K^{-1}\phi + \phi^T S\right)\propto
e^{\frac{1}{2} S^T K S} 
\eeq
Applying this generalized Gaussian integral formula
to the partition function $Z$
for the Ising model, we get
(up to an insignificant factor)


\beq
Z = \int \cald \phi \;
e^{-\frac12\phi^T K^{-1}\phi}
\prod_i \left(\sum_{S_i=\pm1} e^{\phi_i S_i}\right)
\eeq
Since

\beq
\sum_{S_i=\pm1} e^{\phi_i S_i} = 2\cosh(\phi_i)
\eeq
we  get

\beq
Z = \int \cald\phi \;
\exp\left[
-\;\frac{1}{2}\phi^T K^{-1}\phi + \sum_i \ln(2\cosh \phi_i)
\right] 
\eeq
Near criticality, $ \phi-i $ 
for all $i$ is small, so 
we can expand in powers of $\phi_i$

\beq
\ln (2\cosh \phi_i) =
\ln(2) +\frac{\phi_i^2}{2} - \frac{\phi_i^4}{12} + \calo(\phi_i^6)
\eeq
Hence

\beq
Z= \int \cald\phi\; e^{-\cala}
\label{eq-z-feynman-path-first-mention}
\eeq
where

\beq
\cala [\phi] = 
\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\phi_i (K^{-1})_{i,j}\phi_j -
  \sum_i \left(
  \frac{\phi_i^2}{2} - \frac{\phi_i^4}{4(3)} + \cdots
  \right)
\label{eq-cala-matrix}
\eeq
In the the continuum limit,
sums over matrix indices become integrals

\beq
\sum_i \rarrow \int \frac{d^2x}{a^2}
\eeq
Taking the continuum 
limit 
of the matrix expression 
Eq.(\ref{eq-cala-matrix}) for the 
action $\cala$, we get



\beqa
\cala[\phi] &=& \int \frac{d^2 x}{a^2} \left[\frac{\phi}{16\beta J}\left(
1 -\frac{a^2}{4}\sum_\mu \partial_\mu^2\right)\phi-\frac{1}{2}\phi^2 + \frac{1}{4(3)}\phi^4 
\right]
\\
&=&
\int d^2 x \left[-\;\frac{\phi}{8^2\beta J}\left(
\sum_\mu \partial_\mu^2\right)\phi
+\left(\frac{1}{a^2 8\beta J}
-\frac{1}{a^2}\right)\frac{\phi^2}{2} + 
\left(\frac{2}{a^2}\right)\frac{\phi^4}{4!} 
\right]
\label{eq-cala-beta-j-a}
\eeqa
Define $ g$ and $m^2$ by 
\beq
 g = \frac{8}{a^2}\eeq
and

\beq
m^2 = 
\frac{4}{a^2}
\left(
\frac{1-8\beta J}{8\beta J}
\right)
\eeq
If we integrate by parts the first term
of Eq.(\ref{eq-cala-beta-j-a}) to replace $\phi\partial^2_\mu\phi$ by $-(\partial_\mu \phi)^2$,
then we get


\beqa
\cala[\phi]
&=&
\frac{1}{4}
\int d^2x\;
\left[
\frac{1}{(8\beta  J)2}
\sum_\mu\left(\partial_\mu\phi\right)^2 + m^2 \frac{\phi^2}{2}
+  g \frac{\phi^4}{4!}
\right]
\label{eq-cala-pre-rescaling}
\eeqa 
Define

\beq
T_c = 8J,\quad 8\beta J = T_c/T
\eeq
If  we set $8\beta J\approx 1$ in the first term of Eq.(\ref{eq-cala-pre-rescaling}) and
we re-scale $\phi$ and $ g$ according to

\beq
\phi = 2\phi',\quad   g=  g'/4
\eeq
then we get

\beq
\boxed{
 g' = \frac{32}{a^2}}
\eeq

\beq
\boxed{m^2 = \frac{4}{a^2}
\left(
\frac{T-T_c}{T_c}
\right)}
\eeq

\beq
\cala[\phi']=
\int d^2x\;
\left[
\frac12 \sum_\mu(\partial_\mu  \phi')^2
+\frac{m^2}{2}(\phi')^2
+\frac{ g'}{4!}(\phi')^4
\right]
\eeq


To  include  $H$ term in $Z$,
note that
\beq
\ln[2\cosh \phi_i]
\rarrow \ln[2\cosh(\phi_i + \beta H)]
\eeq
and

\beqa
\ln[2\cosh(\phi_i + \beta H)]
&\approx& \ln(2) + \frac12 (\phi_i + \beta H)^2
\\
&\approx&\ln(2) +  \frac{\phi^2_i + (\beta H)^2}{2} + \beta H  \phi_i
\\
&\approx&\ln(2) +  \beta H \phi_i
\eeqa
Hence

\beqa
\sum_i \ln[2\cosh(\phi_i + \beta H)]
&\rarrow&\int \frac{d^2 x} {a^2}\;\beta H \phi
\\
&\rarrow&\int \frac{d^2 x} {a^2}2\beta H \phi'\quad \text{(from $\phi= 2\phi'$ rescaling)}
\\
&=&
\int d^2x\; h \phi'
\eeqa
where

\beq
\boxed{h=\frac{2\beta H}{a^2}}
\eeq


Note that the invariace of the Ising model under $S_i\rarrow -S_i$ gets
transferred to the $\phi^4$  model  as invariance under $\phi \rarrow -\phi$.

Even though the Ising model has no explicit $ S_i^4 $ term because
 $
S_i^4 = 1
 $, the continuum limit
 produces an important $ \phi^4 $ interaction. The $ \phi^4 $   term is r-unstable and marginal,
  higher powers of $\phi$ are   i-stable.

Parallel Ising and $\phi^4$ models exist in any dimension $d$. But the dimension $d=4$ is
  special; it's 
 the   upper critical dimension.
We expand the $\phi^4$ model around $d=4$ because
it  is weakly coupled and thus amenable to
perturbation theory there.
Then we can extrapolate to:
$ d=3 $ (good),
  $ d=2 $ (qualitatively OK, quantitatively inaccurate).

 


\section{Perturbation Theory}

 In Claim $\ref{cl-p4-ising-connection}$, we expressed the  partition function $Z$ as a {\bf Feynman path integral}
\beq
Z= \int \cald\phi\; e^{-\cala}
\label{eq-essence-perturbation-theory}
\eeq
where $\cala$ is 
Ginzburg-Landau action. 
This Feynman path integral
is the essence
of what is called {\bf perturbation
theory with Feynman diagrams}.
If we remove the $\phi^4$ term
from the action, then Eq.(\ref{eq-essence-perturbation-theory}) is just an integral over a  gigantic Gaussian function (GGF), where the GGF lives in an $N$ dimensional space $\{\phi_i\}_{i=1}^N$.
Gaussian integrals are easy to do. The interaction term $\delta \cala=\int d^dx\;g\phi^4/4!$ spoils this Gaussian-ness of the integral, so we expand $\exp(\delta \cala)$ in powers of $\delta \cala$ (=powers of the coupling constant $g$)
and evaluate the moments of a GGF. We don't have time
to delve in detail into 
perturbation theory with Feynman diagrams
in this chapter, 
but suffice it to say that this path integral is the zenith of the whole field.
Everything after that is just coasting  downhill, working out the implications of this realization.

\section{Power counting}



Assume the Euclidean metric.
The Euclidean metric can arise
when you analytically
continue a function of spacetime events $x^\mu=(t, x_1, x_2, x_3)$ so that its time
dependence goes from $t$ to $it$. This
as is sometimes useful when integrating that function.
The Euclidean metric can also arise in a
static system that has achieved steady state,
and its time dependence has
gone away. The latter scenario
is the one pertinent to the 
Ising model and its continuum
approximation, the $\phi^4$ model.

Let

$ \phi(x)$ =  field at point $x\in \RR^d$

$m$ = mass of particle

$ g$ = coupling constant


The bare Lagrangian density,
in the Euclidean metric, for
the $\phi^4$ model in $d$ dimensions is
(bare quantities have a zero subscript)


\beq
\call_0 =
\frac12 \sum_{\mu=1}^d(\partial_\mu \phi_0)^2
+
\frac12 m_0^2 \phi_0^2
+
\frac{ g_0}{4!}\phi_0^4
\eeq

$\call_0$ is a density in $d$ dimensions. Its integral, over
the $d$ dimensional
volume element $d^dx$, is dimensionless: 
\beq
\engdim{\int d^d x \call_0}
=L^0
\eeq


Let
\beq
\epsilon = 4-d
\eeq
Then define


\beq
\phi_0 = Z_\phi^{1/2}\phi(\mu)
\eeq

\beq
m_0^2 = Z_m m^2(\mu)
\eeq

\beq
 g_0 = \mu^\epsilon Z_ g  g(\mu)
\label{eq-lam-bare-mu-eps}
\eeq

\begin{claim}
\beq
\engdim{\phi_0}=\engdim{\phi}= L^{\frac{2-d}{2}},\quad
\engdim{m^2_0}=\engdim{m^2}=L^{-2}
\eeq
\end{claim}
\proof
\beq
L^0=\engdim{(d^dx) (\partial_\mu \phi)^2}
=
L^d L^{-2} \engdim{\phi}^2
\eeq
\qed


\begin{claim}\label{cl-dim-lam}
The factor $\mu^\epsilon$ 
in Eq.(\ref{eq-lam-bare-mu-eps}) 
is introduced to make the coupling constant $ g$
 dimensionless and the bare
coupling constant dimensionful.

\beq
\engdim{ g}=L^0, \quad \engdim{ g_0}= L^{d-4}
\eeq
\end{claim}
\proof

Assume a $\phi^n$
interaction and $\engdim{ g_0}=L^{-p}$. Then

\beq
L^0=
\engdim{(d^dx) g_0 \phi^n_0}=
L^d L^{-p}L^{\frac{n(2-d)}{2}}
\eeq
Hence

\beq
0=d-p+ \frac{n(2-d)}{2}
\eeq
\beq
p= d + n -\frac{nd}{2}
\eeq
If $n=4$,

\beq
p=4-d=\eps
\eeq
If we now set
$\eps= 4-d$,
we get
\beq
p=\eps
\eeq

In the $\phi^3$
theory in 6 dimensions considered
in Srednicki's book Ref.\cite{Sr},
one gets, with $n=3$, that

\beq
p= 3-\frac{d}{2}= \frac{6-d}{2}\eeq
If we now set $\eps =6-d$, we get
\beq
p= \frac{\epsilon}{2}
\eeq
\qed

From Claim \ref{cl-dim-lam},
$\engdim{ g^p_0}=L^{p(4-d)}$.
If multiply $ g^p_0$
  times an integral $\cali$, and assume the
product is dimensionless, then 
 $\cali\sim L^{p(d-4)}$.

\begin{itemize}
 \item $ d < 4 $:
  $  g_0 $ has   positive dimension   so $\phi^4$ term is  r-unstable.
  $\cali$
  converges. Fluctuations
important especially near critical points.
\item  $ d = 4 $:
  $  g_0 $ is   dimensionless  so $\phi^4$ term is marginal. $\cali$
diverges but can be renormalized.

\item  $ d > 4 $:
  $  g_0 $ has negative dimension  so $\phi^4$ term is i-stable.
$\cali$ 
diverges. 
 Fluctuations die, mean-field theory works.
\end{itemize}

\section{Beta Potential}

Newton's equation says 
\beq
m\ddot{x}= F
\eeq
where $m=$mass, $x=$ position, and $F=$ force. But
you could  have, once the acceleration dies down,
an equilibrium between a drag force and a propulsion force. Let

$\gamma \dot{x}=$ drag,  a velocity dependent force with $\gamma>0$

$F=-\partial_x V=$ propulsion, a conservative force with potential function $V$

Then, at equilibrium,

\beq \gamma\dot{x}=F = -\partial_x V
\label{eq-phi-four-drag-prop}
\eeq
and

\beq
\gamma\partial_x\dot{x} = -\partial_x^2 V
\eeq


As shown in Fig.\ref{fig-V-derivatives-phi-four},
$V$ has positive second derivative
(hold water convexity)
on both sides of a {\bf stable, attractive fixed  point (attractor, sink)},
and it has negative second derivative
(drop water convexity)
on both sides of an {\bf unstable, repulsive fixed point (repeller, source)}. Hence, we can think of
$V$ as a potential in which a ball rolls towards an attractor
and away from a repeller.



\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=4in]
{dynamical-sys/V-derivatives.png}
\caption{Illustration of
$\frac{d\dot{x}}{dx}=-\partial^2_xV$. Arrows
in vector field are 
proportional to $\dot{x}$. }
\label{fig-V-derivatives-phi-four}
\end{figure} 

Now 
set
position =$x\rarrow  g$, time=$t\rarrow \ln \mu$,
and $\gamma=1$
in Eq.(\ref{eq-phi-four-drag-prop})

\beq
\frac{d  g}{dt}= \beta( g) = -\partial_ g V
\eeq

\beq
V( g)-V( g_0)
=
-\int_{ g_0}^ g
d g\; \beta( g)
\eeq
We will refer to the function $V( g)$
as the {\bf beta potential}.
For the $\phi^4$ model,
\beq
\beta( g)= -\eps  g + a g^2
\implies V( g)= \frac{\eps}{2} g^2-
\frac{a}{3} g^3 + \text{constant}
\label{eq-pot-p4}
\eeq

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=3in]
{renorm-group-phi-four/beta-potential.png}
\caption{Beta  potential $V( g)$ for $\phi^4$ model.
$time=\ln b$. As  $b$ increases, a ball rolls downward in this potential curve.}
\label{fig-beta-potential}
\end{figure}
The potential given by Eq.(\ref{eq-pot-p4}) is plotted in Fig.\ref{fig-beta-potential}.
It has a minimum at $ g=0$ and a maximum at

\beq
0=\partial_ g V( g^*)=-\beta( g^*)
\implies \quad
 g^*=\frac{\eps}{a},
\quad V( g^*)=\frac{\eps^3}{6a^2}
\eeq
This maximum is called the Wilson-Fisher fixed point
and corresponds to
the Curie point in the Ising model.

Recall that

\beq
\ln b = \ln \mu =\ln\left(\frac{1}{\Lam}\right) 
\eeq
Hence

$b\uparrow$ (coarser grid) $\implies$ $\Lam\downarrow$ ($\rarrow$ UV energies)

 $b\downarrow$ (finer grid) $\implies$ larger $\Lam\uparrow$ ($\rarrow$ Infrared energies)






\section{Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking}

Let

$r=m^2=\propto T-T_c$

$u= g$

Variables are named this way 
because Curie-Wilson-Fisher point at $r=r^*$ and $u=0$
is attractive (i.e., i-stable)
in the $u$ direction and repulsive (i.e., r-unstable) in the $r$ direction.


In this new notation, the Ginzburg–Landau free energy (= Euclidean Action  $\cala$) is

\beq
F[\phi] = \int d^d x \left[ \frac{1}{2}\sum_\mu(\partial_\mu \phi)^2 + \frac{r}{2}\phi^2 + \frac{u}{4!}\phi^4 \right]
\eeq
If we define a $\phi$ potential $V(\phi)$ by

\beq
V(\phi)= \frac{r}{2}\phi^2 + \frac{u}{4!}\phi^4
\eeq
then\footnote{Some people define $u= g/6$ in which case $\phi^* =\sqrt{\frac{-r^*}{u}}$.}



\beq
V'(\phi)=0\implies\quad \phi=0 \text{ or } \phi=\phi^*=\sqrt{\frac{-6r}{u}}
\eeq

\centering
\includegraphics[width=2.3in]
{renorm-group-phi-four/phi-potential.png}
\caption{$\phi$  potential curve $V(\phi)$.}
\label{fig-phi-potential}
\end{figure}
Fig.\ref{fig-phi-potential}
shows a plot of $V(\phi)$.
For $r>0$, it has a single minimum 
at $\phi=0$.
For $r<0$,
it has 2 minima which are 
$\Delta \phi$ apart.

Fig.\ref{fig-r-u-phases}
shows the behavior of the $\phi^4$
theory in the $(r, u)$ plane.

 In the $(r,u)$ plane:

 The region $r>0, u>0$ (top-right quadrant) harbors the disordered phase.
 The region $r<0, u>0$ (top-left quadrant) corresponds to the Mexican hat potential; it harbors 2 possible spontaneous phases (all spins up or all spins down).

The Curie-Wilson-Fisher fixed point (saddle-point) lies at $(r,u)=(0, u^*)$, and a trivial fixed point (maximum) lies at $(r,u)=(0,0)$.

 $r=m^2\propto (T-T_c)$ is a temperature-like c.c. so the $r$ direction is r-unstable, repulsive


 The separation between minima $\Delta \phi \sim \sqrt{|r|/u}$ is constant along lines $u = a r$ with $a<0$.

the 4-point Green's function $\Gamma^{(4)}$ to one-loop order
\beqa
\Gamma^{(4)}&=&
Z_\phi^2\left[
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\ar@{-}[dr]
&
&
\\
& \ar@{-}[ur]\ar@{-}[dr] g_0
&
\\
\ar@{-}[ur]
&
&
}
\ecen
+
\frac{3}{2}
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\ar@{-}[dr]
&
&
&
\\
&\ar@{-}@/_1pc/[r]\ar@{-}@/^1pc/[r]
 g_0
& \ar@{-}[ur]\ar@{-}[dr] g_0
&

\ar@{-}[ur]
&
&
&
}
\ecen

\right]

Z_\phi^2\left[
 g_0+   g_0^2 \frac{3}{2}\cali
\right]
\label{eq-4-point-green-fun}
\eeqa
where the integral $\cali$
in the Euclidean metric
is defined by

\beq
\bcen
\xymatrix@C=3pc{
\ar[dr]|{p_1}
&
&
&

&\ar@/_1pc/[r]|{k+p}\ar@/^1pc/@{<-}[r]|{k}
 g_0

\ar[ur]|{p-p_1}

&

\ecen
\cali = \int \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d}
\frac{1}{(k^2 + m^2)((k+p)^2 + m^2)}
\eeq

\begin{mybluearea}
\begin{claim}\label{cl-cali-fish}
\beq
\cali=
\frac{2}{(4\pi)^2}
\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b_0
-\ln M(p)
\right)
+\calo(\eps)
\eeq

\beq
\ln M(p)= \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 dx\;\ln\left(\Delta\right)
\eeq

\beq
b_0=\frac{1}{2}[\gamma_E -\ln(4\pi)]
\eeq

\beq
\Delta = m^2 + x(1-x)p^2
\eeq
\end{claim}
\noindent \proof

We can apply to $\cali$ the Feynman parameters formula
given by
\beq
\frac{1}{AB} = \int_0^1 dx \frac{1}{[xA + (1-x)B]^2}
\eeq
Doing so yields

\beq
\cali = \int_0^1 dx \int \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d}
\frac{1}{\left[(k + xp)^2 + \Delta\right]^2}
\eeq
If we now shift $k \to k - xp$,
we get

\beq
\cali = \int_0^1 dx \int \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d}
\frac{1}{(k^2 + \Delta)^2}
\eeq
If we next apply to $\cali$ the
following standard formula for dimensional regularization:

\beq
\int \frac{d^d k}{(2\pi)^d}
\frac{1}{(k^2 + \Delta)^2}
=
\frac{1}{(4\pi)^{d/2}}
\Gamma\left(2-\frac{d}{2}\right)
\Delta^{\frac{d}{2} - 2}
\eeq
and set $d = 4 - \epsilon$, we
get


\beq
\cali=
\frac{1}{(4\pi)^{2-\eps/2}}
\Gamma\left(\frac{\eps}{2}\right)
\int_0^1 dx\;
\Delta^{-\frac{\eps}{2}}
\eeq
Next we want to expand
$\cali$ in powers of $\eps$.
To do so we use

\beq
\Gamma\left(\frac{\eps}{2}\right)
\approx
\frac{2}{\eps}-\gamma_E  + \calo(\eps)
\eeq
where $\gamma_E \approx 0.58$ is Euler constant,
and

\beq
\left(\frac{\Delta}{4\pi}\right)^{-\frac{\eps}{2}}
= 1 -\frac{\eps}{2}\ln\left(\frac{\Delta}{4\pi}\right)+\calo(\eps^2)
\eeq
Putting it all together, we get

\beqa
\cali&=&
\frac{1}{(4\pi)^2}
\left(
\frac{2}{\eps} -\gamma_E 
-\int_0^1 dx\;\ln\left(\frac{\Delta}{4\pi}\right)
\right)
\\
&=&
\frac{2}{(4\pi)^2}
\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b_0
-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 dx\;\ln\left(\Delta\right)
\right)
\eeqa

\qed
\end{mybluearea}


\begin{claim}
\beq
Z_ g = 1 + g a\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b\right) +\calo( g^2)
\eeq
where
\beq
a= \frac{3}{(4\pi)^2}
\eeq

\beq
b=
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
0 &
\text{if Minimal Subtraction  (MS)}
\\
b_0&
 \text{if Modified Minimal Subtraction  (MMS)}
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq

\end{claim}
\proof

From Claim \ref{cl-cali-fish},
we get
\beq
\underbrace{\mu^\eps}_{1+\eps \ln\mu}\cali=
\frac{2}{(4\pi)^2}\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b_0 +\ln\left[\frac{\mu}{M(p)}\right]
\right) + \calo(\eps)
\eeq

Let
\beq
Z_g=1+\underbrace{\delta Z_ g}_{\calo(g)}
\eeq
$\delta Z_g$ is called a {\bf counterterm}.
If we express Eq.(\ref{eq-4-point-green-fun})
in terms of counterterms, we get
\beqa
\Gamma^{(4)}
&=& Z_\phi^2\left[
 g_0+   g_0^2 \frac{3}{2}\cali
\right]
\\
&=&[1+\calo( g^2)]\mu^\eps gZ_g^2
[
 \frac{1}{1+\delta Z_g}
+
\mu^{ \eps}g
\frac{3}{2}\cali
]
\\
&=&
1+\calo( g^2)]\mu^\eps g Z_g^2
[
1-\delta Z_g
+\calo((\delta Z_g)^2)+
\mu^{ \eps}g
\frac{3}{2}\cali
]
\eeqa
Now we define $\delta Z_ g$
so as to cancel the $\eps$ pole

\beq
\delta Z_ g
= g\frac{3}{2}
(\mu^{\eps}\cali)_{div}
= g\frac{3}{2}
\frac{2}{(4\pi)^2}\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b
\right)
=a g\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b
\right)
\eeq


\qed


\begin{claim}
If
\beq
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
 g_0 =
\mu^\eps Z_ g\tilde{ g}
\\
\tilde{ g}=
 g[1 + ab g + \calo( g^2)]
\end{array}
\right.
\label{eq-p4-subtract-g-scaled}
\eeq
or

\beq
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
 g_0 =
\tilde{\mu}^\eps Z_ g g
\\
\tilde{\mu}=\mu e^{b}
\end{array}
\right.
\label{eq-p4-subtract-mu-scaled}
\eeq
then $ g_0$ is independent of $b$.
It equals

\beq 
 g_0=\mu^\eps  g
\left[1+
\frac{ g a}{\eps}
 + \calo( g^2)
\right]
\eeq
\end{claim}
\proof

If we assume Eq.(\ref{eq-p4-subtract-g-scaled}), then

\beqa
 g_0 
&=&
\mu^\eps  g
\left[1+
 g a\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b\right)
+ ab g + \calo( g^2)
\right]
\\
&=&
\mu^\eps  g
\left[1+
\frac{ g a}{\eps}
 + \calo( g^2)
\right]
\eeqa
If instead of Eq.(\ref{eq-p4-subtract-g-scaled}),  we assume Eq.(\ref{eq-p4-subtract-mu-scaled}), then
\beq
\tilde{\mu}^\eps=
\mu^\eps\left(1 +\eps b
+\calo(\eps^2)\right)
\eeq
so

\beqa
 g_0 &=& 
\mu^\eps\left(1 +\eps b
+\calo(\eps^2)\right)
\left[
 1 +  g a\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b\right) +\calo( g^2)
\right] g
\\
&=&
\mu^\eps
\left(
 1 +  g a\left(
\frac{1}{\eps}-b\right)
+ g ab
\right) g
\\
&=&
\mu^\eps
\left(
 1 +
\frac{ g a}{\eps}
\right) g
\eeqa

\qed


\begin{claim} 

\beq
\boxed{\beta( g)=
-\epsilon  g
+a g^2}
\eeq


\end{claim}
\proof

We've shown that
\beq
 g_0=
\mu^\eps  g
\left[1+\frac{ g a}{\eps} +\calo( g^2)
\right]
\eeq
Therefore,

\beqa
0&=&\mu\frac{d g_0}{d\mu}
\\
&=&
\mu\frac{d}{d\mu}
\left(\mu^\eps
\left[ g+\frac{ g^2 a}{\eps}
+\calo( g^3) 
\right]\right)
\\
&=&
\eps\mu^\eps
\left[ g+\frac{ g^2 a}{\eps}
\right]
+
\mu^\eps \beta( g)
\left[1+\frac{2 g a}{\eps}
\right]
+
\calo( g^3)
\eeqa
Hence

\beqa
\beta( g)
&=&
-\eps g
\frac{1+\frac{ g a}{\eps}}
{1+\frac{2 g a}{\eps}} +\calo( g^3)
\\
&=&
-\eps g\left(
1-\frac{ g a}{\eps} 
\right)+\calo( g^3)
\\
&=& -\eps g +a  g^2
\eeqa
\qed


Setting
\beq
\beta(  g^*) =0
\eeq
gives the {\bf Wilson–Fisher fixed point}

\beq
  g^* =
\frac{\epsilon}{a}
\approx 52.6 \eps
\eeq
For $d=4$, 
$
\epsilon=0
$
so the fixed point is Gaussian.
For $d<4$, the fixed point is non-trivial and belongs to same the universality class
as the Ising model Curie point.

\section{Appendix C: Critical Exponents}

The critical point of the $\phi^4$ model and that of the Ising model
are
in the same universality class.

One loop results
\beq
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
\gamma_\phi = 0 +\calo( g^2)
\\
\beta( g) = -\eps  g +a  g^2 + \calo( g^3), & (a= \frac{3}{(4\pi)^2})
\\
\gamma_m( g)=a' g +\calo( g^2), & (a'= \frac{1}{2(4\pi)^2})
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq

\begin{claim}
\beq
\nu=
\frac{1}{2}-\frac{a'\eps}{4a} + \calo(\eps^2)
\eeq
\end{claim}
\proof


Near a critical point

\beq
r=m^2 \propto 
\left(\frac{T-T_c}{T_c}\right)
= \theta
\eeq
Hence
\beq
\frac{d\ln r}{d\ln \mu}=\frac{d\ln |\theta|}{d\ln \mu}
\eeq
Also near a  critical point,
the correlation length $\xi$ diverges as a power $\nu$
of $1/|\theta|$.

\beq
\xi = \frac{\xi_0}{|\theta|^\nu}
\eeq
Since also 

\beq
\xi' = \frac{\xi_0}{b}
\eeq
we conclude

\beq
b\propto|\theta|^{\nu}
\eeq
and 
\beq
\gamma_r=
\frac{d\ln r}{d\ln \mu}=
\frac{d\ln | \theta|}{d\ln b}= 
y_\theta = \;\frac{1}{\nu}
\label{eq-anom-dim-nu}
\eeq
Let 
$
\hat{m}=\frac{m}{\mu} 
$
so
\beq
\hat{m}^2_0=\mu^{-2} Z_m r
\eeq
If we assume  $\hat{m}_0^2$
instead of $m_0^2$ is independent of $\mu$, then\footnote{Previously  we assumed $g_0$ and $m_0^2$ 
had non-zero dimensions because
we wanted to obtain only the anomalous contribution when we applied $\frac{d}{d\ln \mu}$ to them. To get both the
engineering and anomalous contributions,
one must divide $g_0$ and $m_0^2$ by $\mu^p$ for some $p$ so as
to make them dimensionless before applying $\frac{d}{d\ln \mu}$ to them.}

\beq
0=\frac{d\ln \hat{m}^2_0}{d\ln \mu}= -2+
\underbrace{\frac{d\ln r}{d \ln\mu}}_{\gamma_r} + 
\underbrace{\frac{d\ln Z_m}{d\ln \mu}}_{-\gamma_m}
\eeq
so
\beqa\gamma_r &=& 2+\gamma_m
\\
&=&
\text{engineering dimension} + \text{ anomalous dimension of $m^2$}
\label{eq-anom-dim-partial}
\eeqa
Comparing Eqs.(\ref{eq-anom-dim-nu}) 
and (\ref{eq-anom-dim-partial}),
we get


\beq
\nu = \frac{1}{2+\gamma_m}
\eeq

At the critical point
of the $\phi^4$ model
\beq
g= g^* = \frac{\eps}{a}
\eeq
and 
\beq
\gamma_m( g^*)= a' g^* = a'\frac{\eps}{a}
\eeq
Hence

\beqa
\nu &=&\frac{1}{2 + \frac{a'\eps}{a}}
\\
&=& \frac{1}{2\left(1+\frac{a'\eps}{2a}\right)}
\\
&=&
\frac{1}{2}-\frac{a'\eps}{4a} + \calo(\eps^2)
\eeqa
\qed

\begin{claim}
\beq
\eta =2 \gamma_\phi( g^*)
= 0 +\calo(\eps^2)
\eeq
\end{claim}
\proof

At the critical point, the connected Green's function $G_c$ scales\footnote{When I say $q$ scales as $b^{y_q}$
or write $q\sim b^{y_q}$, I mean $q' = b^{y_q}q$} as

\beq
G_c\propto \frac{1}{r^{d-2+\eta}}\sim b^{d-2+\eta}
\label{eq-gc-scale-eta}
\eeq

Since
\beq
\engdim{\phi}=M^{\frac{d-2}{2}}
\eeq
we can conclude that

\beq
G_c = \av{\phi, \phi} \sim b^{d-2+2y_\phi}
\label{eq-gc-scale-yp}
\eeq
Comparing Eqs.(\ref{eq-gc-scale-eta}) and 
(\ref{eq-gc-scale-yp}), we 
get

\beq
\eta=2y_\phi
\eeq
Now use

\beq y_\phi=\gamma_\phi = 0 +\calo( g^2)
\eeq
and
\beq
g= g^* = \frac{\eps}{a}
\eeq

\qed
