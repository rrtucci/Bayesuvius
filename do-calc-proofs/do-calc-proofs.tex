\chapter{Do Calculus proofs}
\label{ch-do-calc-proofs}

In Chapter \ref{ch-do-calc},
we explained Do Calculus,
but referred to this
chapter for proofs
of claims that
use Do Calculus.
In this chapter, we've
aggregated
 all proofs, from
throughout the book,
of claims that use Do Calculus.

Note that even though the 3
rules of Do Calculus
are great for proving
adjustment formulae
for general classes of DAGs,
they are sometimes overkill
for proving
 adjustment formulae
for a single specific DAG.
Indeed, since the
 3 rules of Do Calculus
are a consequence
of the d-separation theorem, it follows that
all adjustment
formulae should be
provable from first principles,
assuming only
the d-separation theorem
and the standard rules of
probability theory.
In this chapter, we present
a technique that allows us to do just that: prove 
adjustment formulae from first principles,
without using the 
3 Rules of Do Calculus.

\section{3  Types of Identifiabilities (Invisibilities)}

In this chapter
we  
will replace 
the words

$$\text{ identifiability }
\to \text{ visibility},\quad
\text{ unidentifiability }
\to \text{ invisibility}
$$

$$\text{ identifiable }
\to \text{ visible},\quad
\text{ unidentifiable }
\to \text{ invisible}
$$
because we find 
the latter 
less confusing
and easier to 
pronounce and 
spell.

\subsection{Parameter Visibility}
In Statistics, given a probability distribution
$P(x;\theta)$ that depends on a parameter $\theta$, 
we say  {\bf parameter $\theta$ is visible} iff

\beq
\forall\; \delta\theta \neq 0,\quad \quad
\delta P\neq 0 
\eeq
Hence, the \qt{slope} $\delta P/\delta \theta \neq 0$
for all $\delta\theta$.\footnote{
This is usually stated equivalently as
$\delta P=0$ implies $\delta\theta=0$.} Equivalently, {\bf parameter $\theta$ is 
invisible} 
 iff

\beq\exists \;\delta\theta\in \RR\text{ such that }
\quad\quad \delta P= 0
\eeq

\subsection{Hidden Variable
Visibility}

I will say that hidden variables
in a probability 
distribution $P(\cdot)$
are {\bf partially (resp., totally) invisible} iff 
\beq \text{partially:}\quad
\exists \text{ changes in the 
hidden variables  such that}\quad\quad
\delta P=0
\eeq
\beq
\text{totally:}\quad
\forall \text{ changes in the 
hidden variables,}\quad\quad
\delta P=0
\eeq
Whenever I talk about the
hidden variables
in
$P(\cdot)$ being
visible or invisible
without specifying whether I 
mean partially or totally,
I will be referring to
the total version. 



Let's be more specific about
hidden variable (HV)
invisibility.


Define an
{\bf infinitesimal variation }
$\delta P$ of the
 probability distribution $P(x)$ by

\beq
\delta P(x)=
\delta x\; \pder{P(x)}{x}
\eeq
\beq
\delta_{P_\rvx} \sum_x f(x) P(x)= \sum_x f(x) \delta P(x)
\eeq

This assumes $P(x)$
is a smooth function of
a continuous  $x$.
If we can prove or
disprove HV invisibility
for the smooth $P(x)$ case,
that is strong evidence that
it is true in the discrete case too.


Suppose a bnet $\calg$ with nodes 
$\rvx_1, \rvx_2, \ldots, \rvx_N$ has a joint 
distribution (i.e., promise)
given by
\beq
P(x_{1\dots N})
=\prod_{i=1}^N P(x_i\mid pa(x_i))
\eeq
where $pa(x_i)$ is the empty set for root nodes.
Suppose $H\subset \{1, 2, \ldots, N\}$ is the set of indices belonging to the
hidden variables of the bnet.
Then we define $\calg$  
to be {\bf HV-invisible} iff

\beq
0=\delta_{hidden}P(x_{1\dots N})
=\sum_{h\in H}
\left[\prod_{i\neq h}P(x_i\mid pa(x_i))
\right]\delta
P(x_h\mid pa(x_h))
\eeq  
for all possible 
variations
$\delta P(x_h\mid pa(x_h)$
for all $h\in H$.
Hence, an HV-invisible bnet
is invariant under changes
in the probability
distributions of its hidden variables.

\section{Universal Operations}
Suppose

\begin{itemize} 

\item The promise $\pi_a$ equals:

\beq
\begin{array}{c}
P(z, y, x)
=
P(z|y)P(y|z)P(z)
\\
\\
\xymatrix{
\rvz
&\rvy\ar[l]
&\rvx\ar[l]}
\end{array}
\eeq

\item the promise $\pi_b$ equals:

\beq
\begin{array}{c}
Q(z, y, x)=Q(z|y, x) Q(y| x)Q(x)
\\
\\ 
\xymatrix{
\rvz
&\rvy\ar[l]
&\rvx\ar[l]
\ar@/_1pc/[ll]
}
\end{array}
\eeq
\end{itemize}

Note that 

\beq
\begin{array}{l}
\sum_y P(z|y)P(y|x)= P(z|x)
\\
\\
\xymatrix{
z
&\sum y\ar[l]
&x\ar[l]}=
\xymatrix{z&x\ar[l]}
\end{array}
\label{eq-markovian-op-only}
\eeq
and

\beq
\begin{array}{l}
\sum_y Q(z|y, x)Q(y|x)= Q(z|x)
\\
\\
\xymatrix{
z
&\sum y\ar[l]
&x\ar[l]
\ar@/_1pc/[ll]
}=
\xymatrix{z&x\ar[l]}
\end{array}
\label{eq-universal-op}
\eeq
An alternative notation is to
condition on the promise:

\beq
P(\cdot)= P(\cdot\mid \pi_a),
\quad Q(\cdot)=P(\cdot\mid \pi_b)
\eeq

Note that operation Eq.(\ref{eq-markovian-op-only})
is valid only for a Markovian
promise whereas operation
Eq.(\ref{eq-universal-op})
is a {\bf universal operation}, in the sense that it is valid for any promise.

Do Calculus can succumb to the
following two pifalls:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Making the
promise being considered  ambiguous 
 by neither writing
the conditioning on the promise,
nor using separate symbols $P(\cdot)$ and $Q(\cdot)$.
This does not cause problems
as long as we are dealing
with just one promise.
However, in the case
of Do Calculus,
we are dealing with
at least two
distinct promises,
the original promise (OP),
and its amputated version.
\item Using non-universal operations
to simplify an instantiation.
This removes memory of
how the instantiation was built (modulus universal operations which are not recorded). 
\end{enumerate}
A way to 
avoid these 2 pitfalls is to 
represent instantiations of
a bnet with diagrams, and
to use only 
 universal operations
to simplify a diagram.
Next, we shall discuss
universal operations
in more detail.



\begin{claim}
In any graph, a summed leaf node equals 1:

\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a
P(a|x_1, x_2)f(x_1, x_2, 
\text{anything except $a$})=1
\\
\bcen
\xymatrix@R=.3pc{
x_1\ar[dr]
\\
&\sum a
\\
x_2\ar[ur]
}\ecen=1
\end{array}
\label{eq-collider-sum}
\eeq


\end{claim}
\proof Obvious
\qed

Let us define the
following 2  operations
to be the 
{\bf basic universal
operations}
on a bnet instantiation:
\begin{mybluearea}
\begin{enumerate}
\item
Redirecting an exploding
clique. This includes adding ghost arrows in order to get an exploding 
clique
to begin with.
\item
Adding or removing a summed
leaf node.
\end{enumerate}
\end{mybluearea}

Examples of basic universal operations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item
\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a P(y|a)P(a)=P(y)
\\
\xymatrix{
\EE a\ar[r]
&y}\quad
=P(y)
\end{array}
\label{eq-diff-priors}
\eeq
Furthermore,

\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a P(a|y)P(y)=P(y)
\\
\xymatrix{
\sum a
&\PP y\ar[l]}
\quad=P(y)
\end{array}
\label{eq-diff-priors-ee}
\eeq


\item
\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a P(y|a, x)P(a|x)P(x)=P(y|x)P(x)=P(x,y)
\\
\bcen
\xymatrix@R=1pc{
&\sum a\ar[rd]
\\
\PP x\ar[ru]\ar[rr]
&&y}
\ecen
=
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\PP x\ar[r]
&y}\ecen
\end{array}
\label{eq-med-sum}
\eeq
This follows because the bnet on the left hand side (LHS) is fully
connected so the arrow $\sum a\rarrow y$ can be reversed and then the sum over $a$ yields 1.

The following identity has a similar justification

\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a P(y|x,a)P(x|a)P(a)=P(x,y)
\\
\bcen
\xymatrix@R=1pc{
&\EE a\ar[rd]\ar[ld]
\\
x\ar[rr]
&&y}\ecen
=
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\PP x\ar[r]
&y}
\ecen
\end{array}
\label{eq-med-sum-ee}
\eeq

\item
\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a P(y|a, x_1, x_2)P(a|x_1, x_2)P(x_2|x_1)P(x_1)
= P(y,x_1, x_2)
\\
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\PP x_1\ar[rd]\ar[drr]\ar[dd]
\\
&\sum a\ar[r]
&y
\\
x_2\ar[ru]\ar[rru]
}\ecen
=
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\PP x_1\ar[rrd]\ar[dd]
\\
&&y
\\
x_2\ar[urr]
}
\end{array}
\label{eq-univ-bdoor}
\ecen
\eeq
This follows because the
bnet on the LHS is fully connected, 
so one can reverse the direction of the 
arrow $\sum a \rarrow y$. Then the sum over $a$
yields 1 so the $\sum a$ vertex 
with its 3 incoming arrows can be deleted.

The following identity has a similar justification

\beq
\begin{array}{c}
\sum_a P(y|x_2, a, x_1)P(x_2|a, x_1)P(x_1|a)P(a)
= P(y,x_1, x_2)
\\
\bcen
\xymatrix{
x_1\ar[drr]\ar[dd]
\\
&\EE a\ar[r]\ar[ul]\ar[dl]
&y
\\
x_2\ar[rru]
}\ecen
=
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\PP x_1\ar[rrd]\ar[dd]
\\
&&y
\\
x_2\ar[urr]
}
\ecen
\end{array}
\label{eq-univ-bdoor-ee}
\eeq



\end{enumerate}

\section{Not Do Calculus (NDC) Technique}

The following 2 step heuristic technique,
which I call the {\bf Not Do Calculus} (NDC) technique, appears to be equivalent to Pearl's 3 rules of Do Calculus.\footnote{This NDC technique was invented solely by me. I haven't proven that it is equivalent to Pearl's 
3 rules of Do Calculus. I believe it is,
but it might not be. It 
correctly produces the backdoor and frontdoor adjustment formulae.}

\begin{mybluearea}

\begin{enumerate}
\item 
Equate $P(y\mid \cald\rvx=x)$
to a bnet instantiation
that has a DAG structure identical
to the OP,
except that arrows entering node
$\rvx$ have been amputated.
All nodes  of that instantiation, except
nodes  $x$ and $y$,
are summed over (indicated by $\sum$) if non-root nodes or averaged over
(indicated by $\EE$) if root nodes.



\item Use the basic universal operations to
remove all sums and averages over hidden nodes.
Each sum or average over a hidden node may be 
removed completely or replaced by a sum or average over an observed node. 
\end{enumerate}
Perform these steps until you remove all sums and averages  over hidden variables 
and obtain a valid adjustment formula.
If such an outcome cannot be achieved,
declare the  query visible.

\end{mybluearea}

 for which

is

and do-visible.
Using the NDC technique,
we can easily see why the
query $P(y|\cald\rvx=x)$
is do-invisible
for OP $(a)$ 
and do-visible for OP $(b)$.

For OP $(a)$, after amputating arrow
$z\rarrow x$ and averaging over node $z$,
we get

\beqa
P(y|\cald\rvx=x)
&=&
\bcen
\xymatrix{
\EE z\ar[dr]
\\
x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
\label{eq-simplest-visible}
\eeqa
The right-hand
side of Eq.(\ref{eq-simplest-visible})
is a valid
adjustment formula
because it's expressed in terms
of observed TPMs of $G$.

For bnet $(b)$,
if we amputate arrow
$z\rarrow x$
and average over node $z$,
we get

\beqa
P(y|\cald \rvx =x)
&=&
\bcen
\xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\EE z}\ar[rd]
\\
x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
\label{eq-simplest-do-invisible}
\eeqa
The right-hand
side of Eq.(\ref{eq-simplest-do-invisible})
is not a valid
adjustment formula
because
if we replace $\EE z$ by $\EE x' $, we get 
$\sum_{x'}P(y|x, x')P(x')$,
and $P(y|x, x')$ is undefined.
Furthermore,
there exists a
 $\delta_{hidden}$
such that

\beqa
\delta_{hidden}P(y|\cald \rvx =x)
&=&
\bcen
\xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\delta\EE z}\ar[rd]
\\
x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
\neq 0
\eeqa




\begin{claim}
In an EC, a summed hidden node can be replaced
by a summed non-hidden node:

\beq
\bcen \xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\EE h}
\ar@[red][d]\ar[dr]
\\
x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
=
\bcen \xymatrix{
{\EE z}
\ar@[red][d]\ar[dr]
\\
x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
\label{eq-summed-root-identity}
\eeq
and

\beq
\bcen \xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\sum h}
\ar@{<-}[d]\ar[dr]
\\
\PP x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
=
\bcen \xymatrix{
{\sum z}
\ar@{<-}[d]\ar[dr]
\\
\PP x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
\label{eq-summed-witness-h}
\eeq
\end{claim}
\proof
\beqa
\bcen \xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\EE h}
\ar[d]\ar[dr]
\\
x\ar[r]
&y
}\ecen
&=&
\bcen
\xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\EE h}
\ar[d]\ar[dr]
\ar[r]
&\sum z
\\
x\ar[r]\ar[ur]
&y\ar[u]
}
\ecen
\\
&=&
\bcen
\xymatrix{
*++[F-o]{\sum h}
\ar@{<-}[d]
\ar@{<-}[dr]
\ar@{<-}[r]
&\EE z
\\
x\ar[r]
\ar@{<-}[ur]
&y\ar@{<-}[u]
}
\ecen
\\
&=&
\bcen
\xymatrix{
&\EE z
\\
x\ar[r]
\ar@{<-}[ur]
&y\ar@{<-}[u]
}
\ecen
\eeqa
Eq.(\ref{eq-summed-witness-h})
follows
by redirecting the red arrows
in Eq.(\ref{eq-summed-root-identity}).

\qed
