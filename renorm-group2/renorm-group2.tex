
\chapter{Renormalization Group for
Bayesian Networks}
\label{ch-renorm-group2}

In Chapter \ref{ch-renorm-group1},
we give a general introduction to the
Renormalization Group (RG).
In Chapter \ref{ch-ising-dbnet},
we define an Ising-dbnet (dbnet= Dynamical
Bayesian Network).
In this chapter,
we propose an RG method
applicable to
any bnet.
As an example,
we will
apply our RG method
to the Ising-dbnet.

This chapter and its accompanying software 
{\tt rg4bnet} (see Ref.\cite{rg4bnets}) are original research, first published here.


Our RG method for bnets is analogous
to the real space RG that  is
standard in Condensed Matter Physics. In real space RG,
the RG transformation
takes a cubic lattice
to another cubic lattice
with half as many lattice sites.
What we propose as an RG transformation
for a general bnet $\calg$
is to order the nodes $\rvx_1, \rvx_2, \ldots,\rvx_n$
of $\calg$ in topological order, and 
then to sum over the nodes $\rvx_i$
with even $i$.
In conventional real space
RG, each RG transformation
increases the lattice length
by 2. General
bnets have no length scale
so at first blush, one might think the two methods have little in common.
However, note that real space
RG and bnet RG
both have in common that
their RG transformation
halves the number of nodes (or
lattice sites).

\section{General bnet RG Method}

What we propose as an RG transformation
for a general bnet $\calg$
is to order the nodes $\rvx_1, \rvx_2, \ldots,\rvx_n$
of $\calg$ in topological order, and 
then to sum over the nodes $\rvx_i$
with even $i$.
We will refer to each
halving of nodes as a 
{\bf  decimation step}.
The RG transformation
describes how the coupling
constant $K$ changes to $K'$
during a decimation step.


To test our bnet RG method, let us apply it to
a bnet $\calg$
with six nodes. 
Suppose bnet $\calg$
has six nodes $\rvx^{:6}=
(\rvx_1, \rvx_2, \ldots, \rvx_6)$,
listed in topological
order. Then, the chain
rule for probabilities 
states that\footnote{
Of course, {eq-fully-connected-6-nodes} is
the
fully connected case.
Often, 
many of these arrows 
in Eq.(\ref{eq-fully-connected-6-nodes})
will be missing.}





\beq
\begin{array}{l}
\\
\\
\xymatrix{
\rvx_6
&\rvx_5\ar[l]
&\rvx_4\ar[l]\ar@/_1pc/[ll]
&\rvx_3\ar[l]\ar@/_1pc/[ll]\ar@/_2pc/[lll]
&\rvx_2\ar[l]
\ar@/_1pc/[ll]\ar@/_2pc/[lll]
\ar@/_3pc/[llll]
&\rvx_1
\ar[l]
\ar@/_1pc/[ll]\ar@/_2pc/[lll]
\ar@/_3pc/[llll]
\ar@/_4pc/[lllll]
}
\\
P(x^{:6}) =
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
P(x_6\mid x_5, x_4, x_3, x_2, x_1)
\\
P(x_5\mid x_4, x_3, x_2, x_1)
\\
P(x_4\mid x_3, x_2, x_1)
\\
P(x_3\mid x_2, x_1)
\\
P(x_2\mid x_1)P(x_1)
\end{array}
\right.
\end{array}
\label{eq-fully-connected-6-nodes}
\eeq


Next, we perform the 
following decimation steps

\begin{enumerate}

\item Sum over $x_5, x_3, x_1$.

This leads to
\beq
P(x_6, x_4,  x_2)=
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
P(x_6\mid x_4,x_2)
\\
P(x_4\mid x_2)
\\
P(x_2)
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq
Now we have an odd number
of nodes. But we need to have
an even number of nodes
to perform the next decimation step. 
So we add
 a dummy node $\rvx_2'$
at the beginning of
the topological order.
If 

\beq
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
P(x_2|x'_2)=\delta(x_2, x_2')
\\
P(\rvx_2'=x_2')= P(\rvx_2=x_2')
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq
then

\beq
P(x_6, x_4,  x_2, x_2')=
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
P(x_6\mid x_4,x_2)
\\
P(x_4\mid x_2)
\\
P(x_2\mid x_2')
\\
P(x_2')
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq

\item Sum over $x_4$ and $x_2'$.

This leads to

\beq
P(x_6, x_2)=
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
P(x_6\mid x_2)
\\
P(x_2)
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq

\item Sum over $x_2$ and get $P(x_6)$.
\end{enumerate}

This chain of decimation steps is
easily generalized to
the case where
we start with an arbitrary
number of nodes.
For example, if
we start with
50 nodes, the
number of nodes
at each decimation 
step would be

\beq
50\rarrow (25+1)\rarrow (13 +1)\rarrow (7+1)\rarrow 4\rarrow 2 \rarrow 1
\eeq


In the real space
RG of the 2-dim Ising model
on a cubic  lattice, the RG transformation
$K'(K)$
is always approximate,
because each decimation
generates interactions
of each {\bf decimation-surviving (DS)} spin $S_{surv}$
with spins that are farther 
away from $S_{surv}$ 
than its 
4 nearest neighbors. 
The RG transformation 
assumes that at the 
beginning and at the end
of the transformation,
there are exactly 4 interactions of  $S_{surv}$
with its 4 nearest neighbors,
so one must
neglect interactions generated
by the RG transformation
with next to nearest neighbor spins.

So far, our bnet RG
method is exact.
However, in practice,
we will usually want to
make approximations akin 
to those done in real space RG.

The chain rule for 
probabilities used
above
describes 
a fully connected 
network wherein
the TPM of node
$i$
depends on all $i-1$
previous nodes. However,
usually
we don't start 
with a fully connected 
bnet,
and node $i$
has arrows coming into it
from fewer that 
$i-1$ nodes.
In that case, when
we decimate,
some of the DS nodes
may gain incoming arrows.

During each decimation,
the TPM of one or more of the DS
nodes might end up 
with far too many incoming arrows\footnote{The complexity
of the problem increases
exponentially
with the maximum, over all nodes, of the number of incoming arrows}.
In such cases, we 
can approximate
the TPM
of such nodes
with a TPM with
fewer incoming arrows.
Suppose that a DS
node $\rvx$ has 
incoming arrows
from
nodes $\rva^{:m}$ and  $\rvb^{:n}$,
but the 
interactions 
with $\rvb^{:n}$
are weak and we
would like to neglect
them. In such a case
one can use this
approximation:


\beq
P(x\mid a^{:m}, b^{:n})\rarrow
P(x\mid a^{:m})=
\sum_{b^{:n}}
P(x\mid a^{:m}, b^{:n})
\underbrace{P(b^{:n}\mid a^{:m})}_{\approx \prod_{i=1}^n P(b_i)}
\eeq

To decide
which
incoming arrows
are weak and can
be neglected
without
introducing 
too much error,
we can use the
MI efficiency measure
introduced in Chapter
\ref{ch-ising-dbnet}.
Suppose $\rvx$ is a DS
node and we 
want to decide whether
to neglect or keep 
arrow $\rvc\rarrow \rvx$.
Recall from Chapter \ref{ch-ising-dbnet}
 that the MI efficiency
$\eps(\rvx\larrow \rvc)\in [0,1]$
is defined by
\beq
\eps(\rvx\larrow \rvc)
=\frac{H(\rvx: \rvc)}{H(\rvx)}=
1 -\frac{H(\rvx\mid \rvc)}{H(\rvx)}
\eeq
One strategy
one might 
follow is to
specify a 
threshold $\delta\in[0, 1]$
and define the
arrows $\rvb_j\rarrow \rvx$, 
for all $j$, that we neglect, to 
be the ones with low efficiency

\beq
0\leq \eps(\rvx \larrow \rvb_j)< 1   -\delta
\eeq
and define the
arrows $\rva_i\rarrow\rvx$,
for all $i$, that we keep,
to be the ones with  high efficiency
\beq
1   -\delta <\eps(\rvx\larrow  \rva_i)\leq 1
\eeq

Consider the bnet of Fig.\ref{fig-def-2dim-ising-reused}.
Assume that this is the last time slice of an Ising-dbnet.
Assume that said Ising-dbnet
has gone through 
enough time slices to \qt{equilibrate}, so that
we may safely assume that
by the last time slice,


\beq
S_i^X\approx S_i^Y
\label{eq-X-Y-spins}
\eeq
for all $i$.

Next sum Fig.\ref{fig-def-2dim-ising-reused} over 
$S_5^X$ and $S_5^Y$,
yielding
Fig.\ref{fig-2-dim-ising-sum-s5}
$(a)$. We want
to approximate the bnet
Fig.\ref{fig-2-dim-ising-sum-s5}
$(a)$ by the bnet
Fig.\ref{fig-2-dim-ising-sum-s5}
$(b)$, or, equivalently,
we want to approximate
the left hand side
of Eq.(\ref{eq-approx-dbnet-analytical})
by its right hand side.


\beq\sum_{S_5^X}P_K(S_5^X)
\left\{\begin{array}{l}
P_K(S_4^Y\mid S_5^X, S_1^X, S_7^X)
\\
P_K(S_6^Y\mid S_5^X, S_3^X, S_9^X)
\\
P_K(S_2^Y\mid S_5^X , S_1^X, S_3^X)
\\
P_K(S_8^Y\mid S_5^X, S_7^X, S_9^X)
\end{array}
\right\}
\approx 
\left\{\begin{array}{l}
P_{K'}(S_4^Y\mid  S_1^X, S_7^X)
\\
P_{K'}(S_6^Y\mid  S_3^X, S_9^X)
\\
P_{K'}(S_2^Y\mid  S_1^X, S_3^X)
\\
P_{K'}( S_8^Y\mid S_7^X, S_9^X)
\end{array}
\right\}
\label{eq-approx-dbnet-analytical}
\eeq

The conditional
probabilities in 
Eq.(\ref{eq-approx-dbnet-analytical})
can be expressed
explicitly in terms
of exponentials of $\beta$
times the energy of 
various spin-spin interactions.
For example,\footnote{ $\frac{A_i}{\sum_i num}$
is shorthand for
$\frac{A_i}{\sum_i A_i}$. $num$
stands for \qt{numerator} }

\begin{subequations}\label{eq-explicit-dbnet-tpm}
\beqa
P(S_4^Y\mid S_5^X, S_1^X, S_7^X)
&\approx&
\frac{e^{K S_4^Y(S_5^X + S_1^X + S_7^X)}}
{\sum_{S_4^Y}num}
\\
&=&
\frac{e^{K S_4^Y (S_5^X + S_1^X + S_7^X)}}
{2\cosh[K(S_5^X + S_1^X + S_7^X)]}
\eeqa
and

\beq
P_{K'}(S_4^Y\mid  S_1^X, S_7^X)\approx
\frac{e^{K' S_4^Y(S_1^X +S_7^X)}}
{\sum_{S_4^Y}num}
=
\frac{e^{K' S_4^Y(S_1^X +S_7^X)}}
{2\cosh[K'(S_1^X + S_7^X)]}
\eeq
\end{subequations}
The other 
conditional
probabilities in Eq.(\ref{eq-approx-dbnet-analytical})
have 
explicit expressions
analogous to Eqs.(\ref{eq-explicit-dbnet-tpm}).

Henceforth, we will
drop the $X$ or $Y$
superscripts from the 
spins variables. This 
is justified because of 
Eq.(\ref{eq-X-Y-spins}).

Next, let's define
the following
shorthand notation:

\beq
e^{Ka}=
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
e^{KS_4(S_1 + S_7)}
\\
e^{KS_6(S_3 + S_9)}
\\
e^{KS_2(S_1 + S_3)}
\\
e^{KS_8(S_7 + S_9)}
\end{array}
\right\} 
\eeq



\beq
B_K(S_5)=
\left\{
\begin{array}{l}
2\cosh[K(S_5 + S_1 + S_7)]
\\
2\cosh[K(S_5 + S_3 + S_9)]
\\
2\cosh[K(S_5 + S_1 + S_3)]
\\
2\cosh[K(S_5 + S_7 + S_9)]
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq
and

\beq
\eta = 
S_2 + S_4 + S_6 + S_8
\eeq
In terms of this shorthand
notation, Eq.(\ref{eq-approx-dbnet-analytical}) becomes


\beq
e^{Ka}
\sum_{S_5}
\frac{P(S_5)}{B_K(S_5)}
e^{K\eta S_5}
=
\frac{e^{K'a}}{B_{K'}(S_5=0)}
\label{eq-abridged-approx}
\eeq
If we set

\beq
a \approx 4\;\underbrace{(2\av{S_1})}_\lam \eta=4\lam \eta
\eeq
and 

\beq
P(S_5=\pm 1)=P_\pm
\eeq
then Eq.(\ref{eq-abridged-approx}) becomes

\beq
\frac{P_+ e^{K \eta (1+4\lam)}}
{2^4 \cosh^4(K(+1+\lam))}
+
\frac{P_- e^{K \eta (-1+4\lam)}}
{2^4 \cosh^4(K(-1+\lam))}
=
\frac{e^{K'4\lam\eta}}
{2^4 \cosh^4(K'\lam)}
\label{eq-eta1-div-eta0}
\eeq
If we divide Eq.(\ref{eq-eta1-div-eta0})
by itself with with $\eta=0$,
we get

\begin{subequations}
\label{eq-RG-trans-dbnet}
\beq
\frac{1}{\caln}
\left[
\frac{P_+ e^{K\eta(1+4\lam)}}
{\cosh^4(K(+1+\lam))}
+
\frac{P_- e^{K\eta(-1+4\lam)}}
{\cosh^4(K(-1+\lam))}
\right]
=
e^{K'4\lam\eta}
\eeq
where 


\beq
\caln = 
\frac{P_+}
{\cosh^4(K(+1+\lam))}
+
\frac{P_-}
{\cosh^4(K(-1+\lam))}
\eeq
\end{subequations}

From Eqs.(\ref{eq-RG-trans-dbnet}),
one finds\footnote{So as to prevent algebraic errors, all Taylor
expansions and asymptotic limits
quoted herein 
were obtained
with the symbolic manipulation software {\tt SciPy}.} that as $K\rarrow 0$,
\beq
K' \rarrow K+
K\frac{P_+ - P_-}{4\lam
} + K^2\frac{\eta P_+ P_-}{2\lam}
+\calo(K^3)
\eeq
With $\eta=4$, $P_-=3/4$, $\lam = 1/8$,
this 
becomes

\beq
K'\rarrow 3K^2
\eeq
For those
same values of $\eta, P_-$ 
and $\lam$, we can 
also calculate the
asymptotic 
behavior of 
$K'$ as $K\rarrow \infty$.
We finally obtain,
from Eqs.(\ref{eq-RG-trans-dbnet}), that

\beq
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{As $K\rarrow 0$:} & K' \rarrow 3 K^2
\\
\text{As $K\rarrow \infty$}
& K'\rarrow \frac{5}{2}K -\frac{1}{2}\ln(3)
\end{array}
\right.
\eeq

One can compare 
our results for a 2-dim Ising-dbnet to the
following ones
for the standard 2-dim Ising model. The latter were 
obtained by
real-space renormalization group (RSRG) using a block-spin decimation approximation (BSDA).
\beq
\label{eq-rg-trans-standard}
\begin{array}{l}
K'(K)= 
\frac{3}{8}
\ln \left[
\cosh(4K)\right]
\\
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{As $K\rarrow 0$:}
& K'\rarrow 3K^2 
\\
\text{As $K\rarrow \infty$:}
&
K' \rarrow \frac{3}{2} K
-\frac{3}{8}\ln 2
\end{array}
\right.
\end{array}
\eeq
